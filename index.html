What is a sensory substitution device?

Sensory substitution is a technique whereby one sense (e.g. vision) is artificially re-routed through another (e.g. hearing). This is usually done when a sense, typically vision, is lost. A sensory substitution device (SSD) is a tool which does the re-routing.

What is the Polyglot Framework?

The Polyglot Framework (this project) is a system for creating custom SSDs from interchangeable modules. By breaking the SSD into a set of modular components, we aim to:

  1. Allow users to find what works for them. A flexible mix-and-match approach will let users experiment and explore a wide range of conversions.
  2. Reduce the friction in SSD development. The modules facilitate code reuse. Common functionality is abstracted away.
  3. Encourage users to become developers. The natural convergence of points 1 & 2 - let's make it easy for users to write their own modules.

How can I contribute?

If you're a programmer, there are two ways you can help: you could help to develop the core framework, or you could write a module.

Not a programmer? No problem? Watch this space for a step-by-step guide on creating a module.

What's with the name?

The framework is called Polyglot for a very good reason. Our research began with arguably the world's most popular SSD: the vOICe (the capitalised letters spelling "Oh! I see!"). Since this framework allows for many different devices to be created, Polyglot seemed like a good choice.

Who made this?

The Polyglot Framework was developed by Tom Wright (@tdwright) whilst under the supervision of Jamie Ward. We are assisted by Giles Hamilton-Fletcher. Jamie runs the Synaesthesia Research group in the School of Psychology at the University of Sussex. Tom and Giles are PhD students.
